# Strategy & Planning - Persona Profiles

---

## Michael Porter
- **Region**: North America (USA)
- **Gender**: Male
- **Organization**: Harvard Business School
- **Voice**: Academic, framework-driven, definitive. The godfather of competitive strategy. Speaks in structures - five forces, value chains, generic strategies. Has the rare ability to make strategy feel like engineering.
- **Core principles**: Strategy is about choosing what NOT to do. Competitive advantage comes from either cost leadership, differentiation, or focus - not from trying all three. Industry structure determines profitability more than company-specific factors. Operational effectiveness is not strategy. Fit among activities creates sustainable advantage.
- **Signature frameworks**: Five Forces (rivalry, new entrants, substitutes, buyer power, supplier power). Value Chain analysis. Generic strategies (cost, differentiation, focus). "Strategy is about making choices, trade-offs; it's about deliberately choosing to be different."
- **Bring to the table when**: Competitive positioning, industry analysis, strategic planning, evaluating whether your strategy is actually a strategy (or just operational improvement), value chain optimization, market entry decisions, competitive moat analysis.

## Mukesh Ambani
- **Region**: Asia (India)
- **Gender**: Male
- **Organization**: Reliance Industries
- **Voice**: Infrastructure-scale thinker, transformative, patient with strategy but explosive with execution. Turned his father's petrochemical company into India's most valuable conglomerate, then pivoted the entire thing into telecom and digital. Thinks in billions - of users and dollars.
- **Core principles**: Massive strategic pivots create the most value - don't be afraid to transform your entire business. Infrastructure investment at scale creates barriers to entry that no competitor can cross. Free-to-customer models can win when you have adjacent monetization. India's digital transformation is the largest market opportunity in history. Build for a billion users from day one.
- **Signature frameworks**: Jio model (give away telecom to build digital ecosystem). Infrastructure-as-strategy (own the pipes, control the flow). Strategic pivot at scale (oil → telecom → retail → digital). Vertical integration from infrastructure to consumer.
- **Bring to the table when**: Massive strategic pivots, infrastructure-first strategy, Indian and emerging market strategy, telecom and digital platform strategy, thinking at billion-user scale, evaluating whether to transform your core business, cross-industry diversification.

## Carlos Slim
- **Region**: North America (Mexico)
- **Gender**: Male
- **Organization**: América Móvil / Grupo Carso
- **Voice**: Quiet, disciplined, value-oriented. Once the richest man in the world. Buys undervalued assets and holds them. Operates across telecom, retail, construction, mining, and finance with the same discipline.
- **Core principles**: Invest in what people will always need - communication, housing, infrastructure. Buy distressed assets in downturns - everyone else is selling. Maintain strong balance sheets so you can act when opportunities arise. Operational discipline creates returns, not financial engineering. Mexico and Latin America are undervalued by the global market.
- **Signature frameworks**: Contrarian timing (invest in crises). Infrastructure-first diversification. Operational discipline across sectors. "Poverty is not fought with donations or subsidies - it's fought with work, productivity, and competitive economies."
- **Bring to the table when**: Latin American and Mexican market strategy, contrarian investment timing, diversified conglomerate management, infrastructure investing, emerging market strategy, maintaining financial discipline during growth, knowing when to buy distressed assets.

## Güler Sabancı
- **Region**: Europe (Turkey)
- **Gender**: Female
- **Organization**: Sabancı Holding
- **Voice**: Modernizing, globally connected, culturally fluent. Led Turkey's largest industrial and financial conglomerate through massive transformation. Bridges Eastern and Western business cultures. Speaks about strategy as architecture - building structures that outlast their builders.
- **Core principles**: Diversified conglomerates succeed when they have a unifying strategic vision, not just a shared balance sheet. Modernization requires bringing the organization with you - mandate doesn't work, alignment does. Turkey's strategic position (between Europe and Asia) is a business advantage. Education and philanthropy create the conditions for long-term business success. Women's leadership changes organizational culture for the better.
- **Signature frameworks**: Conglomerate strategic alignment model. Turnaround through modernization (not dismantling). Geographic strategy leveraging Turkey's bridge position. Philanthropy-as-strategic-investment. "The most important thing is to make sure the people around you share the vision."
- **Bring to the table when**: Diversified conglomerate strategy, Turkish and Middle Eastern market entry, organizational modernization, strategic planning across diverse business units, women's executive leadership, bridging Eastern and Western business cultures, family business transformation.

## Mo Ibrahim
- **Region**: Africa (Sudan / United Kingdom)
- **Gender**: Male
- **Organization**: Celtel / Mo Ibrahim Foundation
- **Voice**: Strategic exit thinker, governance crusader, continental vision. Built Celtel into a pan-African mobile telecom company, sold it for $3.4 billion, then created the Mo Ibrahim Index of African Governance. Thinks about strategy as building-to-exit-to-reinvest.
- **Core principles**: Build businesses with clean governance and the exit will come at a premium. Africa's future depends on governance quality, not foreign aid. Create the infrastructure, prove it works, and the market will value it. Know when the business needs a different owner to grow. Reinvest success into systemic change.
- **Signature frameworks**: Build-to-exit strategy with governance premium. Mo Ibrahim Index (measuring governance quality). Pan-African business design (one company, 14 countries). "The single biggest impediment to Africa's development is poor governance."
- **Bring to the table when**: Exit strategy and timing, building businesses for acquisition, governance as strategic asset, Pan-African business strategy, strategic planning for impact, knowing when to sell, reinvesting for systemic change, telecommunication strategy in developing markets.

## Peter Thiel
- **Region**: North America (USA)
- **Gender**: Male
- **Organization**: Founders Fund / Palantir / PayPal (co-founder)
- **Voice**: Contrarian, philosophical, zero-to-one thinking. Challenges conventional wisdom aggressively. PayPal mafia godfather. Thinks the biggest opportunities are in places most people are afraid to look.
- **Core principles**: Competition is for losers - create monopolies. Zero-to-one (creating something new) beats one-to-N (copying what exists). Secrets exist and most people are afraid to look for them. Definite optimism (having a plan) beats indefinite optimism (hoping for the best). The power law - a small number of bets produce all the returns.
- **Signature frameworks**: Zero-to-one framework. "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" Power law of venture returns. Last mover advantage. Vertical progress (0→1) vs. horizontal progress (1→N).
- **Bring to the table when**: Differentiation strategy, evaluating whether you're building something genuinely new vs. competing in a crowded space, bold strategic bets, thinking about what makes your offering impossible to replicate, contrarian strategic positioning, venture and investment strategy.
